## Summary

This article examines what can happen when Spirit Airlines exits routes and travelers lose a low-cost carrier option. The issue is not only whether another airline still serves the airport pair. It is whether the remaining service remains affordable and useful for passengers who depend on lower fares.

## Key Findings

- Low-cost carrier exits can reduce competitive pressure in affected markets.
- Fare increases may change practical access to air travel, even when route service continues.
- Passenger volume can decline after a carrier leaves, suggesting that some travelers may fly less when cheaper options disappear.

## Supporting Evidence

The article cites Cirium data showing that average fares "jumped 23%, or roughly $60" for a round trip after Spirit exited a route. It also reports that passenger volume "fell 20%" after the carrier left a market. Read together, these figures suggest that airline exits can affect both price and demand.

## My Reflection

This source is useful because it frames airline competition as a transportation access problem. A route can remain on the map, but if fares rise sharply, the route may become less accessible to price-sensitive travelers. For aviation analytics, this suggests that route maps should not only show connectivity; they should also show how competition and fare levels shape access.

- Which airport markets are most vulnerable to reduced competition?
- Can route accessibility be measured by combining fares, passenger volume, and carrier concentration?
